The environmental effects of urban blue and green space

 

Sponge city planning research in the view of landscape analysis

In the new normal period of economic transition in China, The Government came up with the concept of the Sponge City, we have some different views of sponge city planning The research extracted the impervious surface from the non-NGI(natural green infrastructure ) landscape and used the precipitation-volumetric method to simulate waterlogging scenarios. We determined the area of rainwater submersion by simulation experiments, the change in the NGI landscape by overlaying images of the region, and the association between these features by data mining. Using the change in ecology indices, we ascertained the changing spatial patterns of the NGI landscape and used these findings to formulate suggestions for Sponge City planning in Fuzhou. Overall, our work combines three research themes, urban waterlogging, landscape ecology, and data mining, for a deep analysis of the relationship between the NGI landscape and storm runoff in an urban environment.

How green space influence the fine particulate matter and urban heat island effect

The spatial distribution of green space will influence the fine particulate matter, land surface temperature, and other urban ecological process. The influence include direct and indirect effects. How to quantify the relationship between green space and environmental effects is so important. We combine ground observations, remote sensing and spatial statistics models to determine it. The results can provide some suggestions for policy makers.

Results

  • PM2. 5 Spatiotemporal Evolution and Drivers in the Yangtze River Delta between 2005 and 2015

The spatial distribution of changes in annual average PM2.5 concentrations in different YRD cities from 2005 to 2015

The proportion of level V concentrations continuously increased from 0.00% in 2005 to 7.75% in 2015, showing a rapid expansion trend in the YRD. The proportion of level I and level V concentration gradually increased from 2005 to 2010.

Annual average PM2.5 concentrations in different climate, ecosystem and geomorphological subregions (NS = North subtropical humid zone, CS = Central subtropical humid zone, AL = Arable land ecosystem, FL=Forest ecosystem, GL = Grassland ecosystem, WL = Water and wetland ecosystem, HS = Human settlement ecosystem, PL = Plain, TL = Tableland, HI = Hills, Mo1 = Small-undulating mountain, Mo2 = Mid-undulating mountain, Mo3 = Large-undulating mountain)

For the years 2005, 2010 and 2015, average PM2.5 concentrations were highest in the subregion of the human settlement ecosystem , followed by the farmland ecosystem and the wetland ecosystem . Average PM2.5 concentrations were highest in the north subtropic humid climate subregion and the central subtropic humid climate subregion . And finally, average PM2.5 concentrations were highest in the geomorphological plain subregion, followed by the platform subregion and the hilly subregion

  • Determining the Mechanisms that Influence the Surface Temperature of Urban Forest Canopies by Combining Remote Sensing Methods, Ground Observations, and Spatial Statistical Models

Spatial distribution of temperature in 2004 and 2014 obtained using a 2250 m threshold and local Getis–Ord Gi statistics. Red (GiZscore ≥ 1.65) indicates a hot spot, blue (GiZscore ≤ −1.65) indicates a cold spot, yellow (1.65 > GiZscore > −1.65) indicates a nonsignificant area, and gray indicates altitude. The numbers indicate the main regions of mutual conversion of cold spots, hot spots, and nonsignificant areas

The aggregated STUFC areas (absolute value of Z score ≥ 1.65 represents statistical significance) identified through the spatial statistical method were more suitable than the non-aggregated areas (1.65>Z Score>−1.65) to determine the relationship and mechanisms.

Influence (q value) of interactions in cold spot areas in 2004 and 2014

The environmental factors with interactions varied during different time periods. For instance, for the cold-spot area, the major interaction factors changed from DS in 2004 to ISP100 in 2014.

  • Individual and interactive influences of anthropogenic and ecological factors on forest PM2. 5 concentrations at an urban scale

Spatial distributions of urban forest PM2.5 concentrations on three different days at the optimal distance threshold of 3500 m

The spatial distributions of PM2.5 concentrations on 13 and 29 December 2014 and 14 January 2015 showed significant spatial clustering. The aggregated areas (hot spots and cold spots) of PM2.5 identified by Getis-Ord Gi* showed that PM2.5 hot spots were concentrated in urban centers and suburbs and cold spots were mainly distributed in the suburbs and exurban regions.

Scores for the interactive effects of impact factors sensitivity rankings at the three periods studied calculated based on simulations using multisource data (A is the result on 13 December 2014; B is the result on 29 December 2014; C is the result on 14 January 2015)

With the acceleration of urbanization, the interactive effects of population density, impervious surface percentage, and other factors could result in increased air pollution.
